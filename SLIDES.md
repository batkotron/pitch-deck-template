# The 10 Slides

The example throughout is **Loomstack**, a fictional Australian SaaS startup helping engineering teams track on-call workload. Use it as a reference, not a script - your story will be different.

---

## Slide 1: Title

**One line: who you are, what you do, who it's for.**

> **Loomstack**
> The on-call workload tracker for engineering teams.
> Series A | Sydney, Australia

That's it. No tagline. No mission statement. No logo wall. Investors are not impressed by the title slide; they just need to know what they're about to see.

**What to include:**
- Company name (large)
- One-line description (clear, no jargon)
- Round you're raising (optional but useful)
- Founder name + email (small, bottom)
- Date

**What to cut:** Logos of "as featured in" press, mission statements, decorative imagery, your slogan.

---

## Slide 2: Problem

**Make the investor feel the pain. Be specific. Be visceral.**

> **Engineering teams burn out their best people on-call.**
>
> - 67% of senior engineers say on-call is the #1 reason they consider leaving.
> - Most teams have no visibility into who carries the weight - it lives in spreadsheets and Slack threads.
> - When the best engineer quits, the team's reliability collapses for 6-9 months.
>
> **The teams who solve this keep their best people. The teams who don't lose them.**

**What works:**
- A specific, named pain (not "communication is hard")
- Numbers from research or your own customer interviews
- A consequence the investor cares about (cost, retention, growth, churn)

**What doesn't:**
- "Everyone struggles with X" - too broad
- Personal anecdotes without data backing them up
- Listing five problems instead of one big one

---

## Slide 3: Solution

**One sentence on what you built. Then show it.**

> **Loomstack tracks every minute of on-call work and flags burnout risk before it happens.**
>
> [Screenshot of the product dashboard]
>
> Connects to PagerDuty, Opsgenie, GitHub, and Slack in 3 clicks.

**Rule:** if your solution slide takes more than 30 seconds to understand, it's broken. Investors are skimming. Your solution should be obvious from a screenshot and a sentence.

**What to cut:** technical architecture, "how it works" diagrams (save for the appendix), the words "leveraging," "synergy," and "powered by AI" unless AI is genuinely the point.

---

## Slide 4: Market

**Bottom-up sizing, not top-down. Show your maths.**

> **$4.2B addressable market in ANZ + US.**
>
> - 180,000 engineering teams in ANZ + US with 10+ engineers (PitchBook, 2025)
> - Average team carries $130K/year in on-call cost (salary loaded + tooling)
> - We capture $200/engineer/month at scale = $24K/team/year
> - 180K teams x $24K = $4.3B TAM

**What investors want:** to believe you've thought about this. The exact number matters less than the methodology.

**Bad version:** "The DevOps tooling market is $50B and growing 25% YoY." (top-down, generic, lazy)

**Good version:** the bottom-up calculation above. Show your assumptions. Investors will challenge them - that's good. They'll trust the ones they can argue with.

---

## Slide 5: Product

**Screenshots. Big ones. Captions that show value.**

> [Screenshot 1: dashboard showing on-call workload across team]
> *"See who's carrying the load - in real time."*
>
> [Screenshot 2: burnout risk alert]
> *"Catch burnout 4 weeks before it happens."*
>
> [Screenshot 3: integration page]
> *"Connects in 3 clicks. No engineering work required."*

Three screenshots is the sweet spot. Each gets a one-line caption that explains the value, not the feature.

**Bad caption:** "Real-time analytics dashboard with custom widgets."
**Good caption:** "See who's carrying the load - in real time."

The difference: features describe what it does, value describes what changes for the user.

---

## Slide 6: Traction

**This is the slide investors flip to first. Make it count.**

> **From $0 to $47K MRR in 11 months.**
>
> [Hockey stick chart: MRR by month]
>
> - 78 paying teams, including Atlassian, Canva, and SafetyCulture
> - 4.2% logo churn (industry benchmark: 6%)
> - $1,180 CAC, 14 month payback
> - 31% MoM growth, 6 months running

**What works:**
- One headline number (the chart's title)
- 3-5 supporting stats that triangulate the headline
- Logos of recognisable customers (only if they're real and on a paid plan)
- A clear chart showing the trajectory

**Honesty rules:**
- Don't show MRR if you're pre-revenue. Show usage. Show retention. Show what you have.
- Don't extrapolate fake growth. Investors can smell it.
- If you don't have traction, your slide order changes - traction moves to slide 9, team and vision move up.

---

## Slide 7: Business Model

**How you make money, and why the unit economics work.**

> **$200/engineer/month, billed annually.**
>
> - Average team: 12 engineers = $28,800 ACV
> - Gross margin: 87%
> - LTV/CAC: 4.2x
> - Net retention: 119% (expansion as teams grow)
>
> **We make more from each customer every year they stay.**

Investors care about three things on this slide:
1. **Pricing** - is it big enough to support enterprise sales?
2. **Margins** - is this a software business or a services business in disguise?
3. **Expansion** - can existing customers spend more next year?

The last point is the most important. Net retention above 110% is the difference between a good business and a great one.

---

## Slide 8: Competition

**Show you've thought about it. Show why you win.**

> | | Loomstack | PagerDuty | Opsgenie | Spreadsheets |
> |---|---|---|---|---|
> | On-call alerting | Yes | Yes | Yes | No |
> | Workload tracking | Yes | No | No | Manual |
> | Burnout detection | Yes | No | No | No |
> | 3-click setup | Yes | No | No | - |
> | Built for engineering culture | Yes | No | No | - |
>
> **PagerDuty fights fires. Loomstack prevents the people who fight them from quitting.**

**Two formats that work:**
1. **Comparison table** (above) - works when you have a clear feature differentiator
2. **2x2 matrix** - works when you have a clear positioning differentiator

**Anti-pattern:** the "we have no competition" slide. You always have competition. Even if it's spreadsheets, even if it's "doing nothing." Investors think founders who say "no competition" haven't done their homework.

---

## Slide 9: Team

**Why you, why now.**

> **The team that's been the on-call engineer.**
>
> **Sarah Chen, Co-founder & CEO**
> Ex-VP Engineering at Linktree. Ran on-call for 60 engineers. Built the internal tool that became Loomstack.
>
> **James Patel, Co-founder & CTO**
> Ex-Staff Engineer at Atlassian. 8 years on call across Bitbucket and Jira platforms.
>
> **Advised by:** Mike Cannon-Brookes (Atlassian), Melanie Perkins (Canva)

**What works:**
- Each founder gets a one-line bio that proves they're the right person for THIS specific company
- Advisors are listed only if they're actively involved (investors will check)
- Photos optional - clean slides often skip them

**Anti-pattern:** listing every employee. The team slide is for founders and key hires only.

---

## Slide 10: Ask

**What you want. What you'll do with it. What it gets investors.**

> **Raising $4M Series A.**
>
> **18 months runway to:**
> - Hit $200K MRR (4.2x current)
> - Open US sales office (NYC)
> - Ship enterprise tier (SOC2, SSO, audit logs)
>
> **Lead investor sought. Existing investors (Blackbird, Antler) committed for 30%.**
>
> **Sarah Chen | sarah@loomstack.com | loomstack.com**

**The four things this slide must include:**
1. **Amount** - exact number, not "$3-5M"
2. **What it buys** - specific milestones, not vague phases
3. **Existing investors** - commitments build momentum
4. **Contact** - one email, not a contact form

**Anti-pattern:** the "thank you" slide. Investors don't need thanks; they need to know how to get back to you.

---

## Bonus: Appendix slides

Investors who get this far want detail. Have these ready but not in the main flow:

- Detailed financials (3-year P&L)
- Detailed product roadmap
- Customer case studies
- Hiring plan
- Cap table summary
- Existing investor list
- Press / awards (only if Tier 1)
